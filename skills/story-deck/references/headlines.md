# Headlines Reference

Use this reference for full storyboard, critique, rewrite, Marp, or PPTX work.

## Core Rules

- Write headlines as memorable lines, not labels.
- A strong headline reveals the slide's point. It does not merely describe the slide topic.
- Every slide title must be strong and easy to read: plain words, one idea, active voice, no jargon stack, no nested clauses, and no cleverness that slows comprehension.
- Prefer 8-14 words for normal slide titles.
- Never exceed 18 words unless the user explicitly asks for dense consulting titles.
- Do not confuse catchy with vague. A catchy line must still make a clear business claim.
- Do not confuse emotional with exaggerated. The goal is narrative force, not melodrama.

## Intensity Scale

Use the right level for the audience:

- **Level 1 - Plain Business:** clean, safe, direct.
- **Level 2 - Sharp Executive:** clearer point of view and stronger contrast.
- **Level 3 - Cinematic-Business:** memorable and vivid, but still credible.

Strategic decks should mostly use Level 2 and selective Level 3. Level 1 is safe but often forgettable. Level 3 is powerful but should not be overused.

## Headline Options

For important slides, produce three headline options:

- Plain Business
- Sharp Executive
- Cinematic-Business

Then recommend the strongest option and explain why it wins on clarity, tension, and memorability.

## Title Refinement Pass

Before showing a slide-title approval list, refine every proposed title once. Present the refined list, not the weaker first draft.

Use this rewrite formula:

```text
domain noun + concrete contrast or causal chain + business consequence
```

Apply these checks:

- Name the world early. Use the audience's domain nouns in the first few titles: product, market, workflow, customer type, account type, system, or decision context.
- Prefer concrete business nouns over abstractions. Use nouns like accounts, contacts, notes, tasks, queues, approvals, evidence, workspaces, margin, churn, renewal, pipeline, or decision when they fit the source material.
- Turn abstraction into contrast. Prefer "Notes show what happened, but not what should happen next" over "Notes capture complexity."
- Make each Act II anchor an argument, not product history. Avoid anchors that mainly say `V0 did X`, `V1 did Y`, `V2 does Z`; use version history only as supporting evidence.
- Make AI behavior accountable. If AI appears in a title, connect it to human confirmation, source evidence, traceability, governance, or committed work.
- Prefer causal chains when the story is about workflow. Titles should show how one thing leads to another: signal -> recommendation -> confirmation -> task -> action -> outcome.
- Make the core question useful, not clever. It should name the decision the audience needs to make.
- Make the call to action name the work, not only the approval. If approval matters, combine it with the concrete thing being approved.
- Cut generic words unless grounded in a specific workflow: complexity, context, value, capability, efficiency, scalable, seamless, AI-native, business-aware, transformation.
- Keep the line easy to read. If a sharper title becomes too dense, choose the plainer version.

Examples:

| Weak first draft | Stronger refinement |
|------------------|---------------------|
| Relationship work has outgrown simple follow-up habits | In franchise CRM, the relationship is the business |
| Notes capture complexity, but tasks organize action | Notes show what happened, but not what should happen next |
| V1 made notes central, but action still stayed manual | Manual task entry is the baseline, not the breakthrough |
| AI recommends; humans confirm; tasks track execution | Recommendations become tasks only when people confirm the work |
| Are we adding tasks or managing complexity? | What should task management become in V2? |
| Approve V2 as AI-native task management | Build the chain from notes to committed work |

## Contrast Patterns

Prefer contrast-driven headlines:

- "Notes record what happened. Memory explains what matters now."
- "The CRM has history, but no judgment."
- "AI cannot help if every account conversation starts from zero."
- "What we store is not the same as what we understand."

## Anti-Corporate Language

Avoid sterile phrases:

- "improve operational efficiency"
- "leverage data assets"
- "enhance decision support"
- "optimize customer engagement"

Replace them with clear human lines:

- "The data exists, but the context disappears."
- "AI can search history, but it cannot hold the account in mind."
- "Memory turns scattered history into current judgment."

## Tests

The best slide line should make the audience think at least one of these:

- "That's true."
- "That's the real problem."
- "I hadn't seen it that way."
- "That is the choice we have to make."
- "That is the line I'll remember."

Remove generic setup phrases, balanced-but-empty wording, and abstract business filler so the deck does not sound like AI wrote it.
